06 Recommended Learning Priority Stack
1
Linear Algebra + Calculus — Deep, Not Surface
Re-learn LA through Strang's MIT lectures and Axler's "LADR". Go beyond "matrices = weights" — internalize SVD, eigenspaces, and tensor operations. You'll use this every single day. For calculus, really understand Jacobians and the chain rule mechanistically — this IS backpropagation.
2
Probability, Statistics & Information Theory Together
Study these concurrently. Blitzstein's probability book + Cover & Thomas "Elements of Information Theory". Understanding KL divergence, cross-entropy, and ELBO at a deep level unlocks VAEs, diffusion models, RLHF reward modeling, and loss function design from first principles.
3
Systems Programming — C++, CUDA, Memory Model
Pick up "Programming Massively Parallel Processors" (Kirk & Hwu). Write actual CUDA kernels — a naive matmul, then an optimized tiled one. Understand cache utilization. This separates researchers from engineers who can actually ship fast models.
4
DSA With AI Context — Not Just LeetCode
Study DSA but always ask: "where does this show up in an ML system?" Dynamic programming in CTC decoding, graph traversal in AutoML, HNSW graphs for vector databases. Read the vLLM, FlashAttention, and Megatron-LM source code to see DSA in production AI.
5
Optimization Theory + Numerical Methods
Boyd & Vandenberghe "Convex Optimization" is the bible. Understand why Adam works via adaptive curvature, why loss landscapes are non-convex but still trainable, and how numerical precision affects training at scale. This makes you dangerous when debugging training instabilities.
6
Physics — Statistical Mechanics + Signal Processing
Feynman lectures on statistical mechanics + Oppenheim's signal processing. The Boltzmann/softmax connection is not superficial — it gives you a physical intuition for why temperature scaling in LLMs works, and why certain loss landscapes resemble energy landscapes in physics.
